One of the causes of death among countries is cardiovascular diseases, which is the cause of almost 18 million deaths every year [1]. Identifying people who are at high risk of developing heart disease early is consequently important to enhance clinical outcomes and avoid death. Over the last few years, machine learning (ML) methods have been applied in the medical field with growing frequency because of their capacity to analyze complicated clinical data and spot trends that could have been obscured by conventional statistical methods.
It has been previously proved that ML models are effective in the prediction of heart diseases based on organized clinical data. Logistic regression and decision trees are traditional algorithms that are often employed because they can be interpreted and their performance remains consistent when applied in a medical decision-support system [21]. Ensemble learning methods especially random forest model has demonstrated better predictive performance in that they capture nonlinear relationships among risk factors of cardiovascular risk [22]. The application and use of Support Vector Machines (SVMs) has also proven to be highly used and competitive with the proper scaling of features and kernel optimization strategies [23].
Current literature reminds that data preprocessing, feature selection, and validation techniques are key determinants of the success of ML-based prediction models and not algorithm selection itself [24]. Consequently, enhanced focus has been placed on open and reproducible ML pipelines with standardized preprocessing strategies and powerful evaluation strategies like stratified k-fold cross-validation [25].
Here, the analysis will be quantitative, based on secondary data in the form of publicly available datasets of heart disease provided in Kaggle on the basis of the UCI Cleveland database. This research employs logistic regression, decision tree, random forest, and support tree machine classifiers to understand the effect of influential clinical characteristics and contrast the performance of predicting the outcome in a constant preprocessing and validation environment.
